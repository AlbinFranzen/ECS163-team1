Afghanistan
Ongoing conflicts, civil war, and Taliban rule were primary emigration drivers, leading to mass refugee flows, mainly to Pakistan and Iran. Immigration was largely limited to returning refugees during calmer periods and international personnel. Post-2001 intervention triggered new displacements and some repatriations.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/afghanistan_migration_profile_2010.pdf (See "Historical migration trends" section for context)

Albania
The collapse of communism triggered mass emigration due to severe economic crisis and political instability, primarily to Italy and Greece. Immigration remained minimal, with some transit migration. The period saw a shift from chaotic exodus to more regular, though often undocumented, labor migration.
Source: https://www.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl486/files/jahia/webdav/shared/shared/mainsite/policy_and_research/migration_albania_country_profile_2008.pdf

Algeria
Economic hardship, high unemployment, and civil conflict (1990s) drove emigration, primarily to France and other European countries. Immigration was limited, mainly some sub-Saharan African migrants in transit or seeking work. The end of intense conflict saw some reduction in asylum-driven departures.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/algeria-migration-crossroads (Provides historical context covering the period)

Andorra
Sustained immigration from Spain, France, and Portugal was driven by Andorra's prosperous tourism and finance sectors. Emigration remained low, typically for specialized education or careers. The period maintained Andorra's status as a country with a high foreign-born population, reflecting consistent economic attraction.
Source: https://rm.coe.int/third-report-on-andorra/16808b5586 (ECRI 2007 report, see p.9 on population composition implying economic drivers)

Angola
Prolonged civil war (until 2002) was the primary driver of emigration, creating large refugee populations in neighboring countries. Immigration was minimal during the war but saw an increase post-2002 with returning refugees and some foreign investment-linked labor. The major shift was from conflict-driven outflow to post-conflict return.
Source: https://www.internal-displacement.org/sites/default/files/publications/documents/2009-angola-country-profile-cp.pdf (Context on conflict-driven movement)

Antigua and Barbuda
Emigration, particularly of skilled individuals ("brain drain"), was driven by pursuit of opportunities in North America and Europe. Immigration consisted of labor for tourism and construction, mainly from other Caribbean nations. Economic shifts in tourism and hurricane impacts periodically affected both flows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (See pages 73-76 for Antigua and Barbuda)

Argentina
Economic crises, particularly in the late 1990s and early 2000s, fueled emigration of professionals and youth, mainly to Spain, Italy, and the US. Immigration continued from neighboring countries (Bolivia, Paraguay, Peru) for labor. The 2001 crisis marked a peak in emigration.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/argentina-once-great-immigration-success-story (Discusses historical trends including the 2001 crisis impact)

Armenia
Post-Soviet independence, the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict, and severe economic decline drove mass emigration, primarily to Russia. Immigration was low, mainly ethnic Armenians from conflict zones or diaspora. Economic hardship remained a key emigration driver.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/iom_armenia_assessment_2010.pdf

Australia
Skilled migration programs and family reunification fueled robust immigration from diverse global sources, particularly Asia. Emigration of Australians (often skilled) also occurred, primarily to the UK and US. Policy shifts fine-tuned skill categories, maintaining high immigration levels.
Source: https://www.aph.gov.au/About_Parliament/Parliamentary_Departments/Parliamentary_Library/pubs/rp/rp1011/11rp05 (Sections cover trends in this period)

Austria
EU accession (1995) and Balkan conflicts shaped migration. Immigration included labor from new EU states (post-2004), asylum seekers from Yugoslavia, and family reunification. Emigration was modest, mainly skilled Austrians to other EU countries. Policy focused on managing integration and asylum claims.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/austria_migr_outlook-2011-10-en (Austria chapter)

Azerbaijan
The Nagorno-Karabakh conflict led to large-scale IDP populations and emigration. Economic transition and opportunities in Russia also drove labor emigration. Immigration was limited, some returning Azerbaijanis and minor labor/transit migration. Post-ceasefire, focus shifted from conflict to economic drivers.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_profile_azerbaijan_2009.pdf

Bahamas, The
Economic opportunities in tourism and financial services attracted immigrants, primarily from Haiti and Jamaica. Emigration of Bahamians was mainly for higher education and professional roles abroad, often to the US. Stricter immigration enforcement and economic fluctuations influenced inflow rates.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (See pages 77-80 for The Bahamas)

Bahrain
Significant labor immigration, primarily from South and Southeast Asia, fueled construction and service sectors due to oil wealth and economic diversification. Emigration of Bahraini nationals was low. The system relied heavily on temporary foreign workers.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, Bahrain country profile)

Bangladesh
Large-scale labor emigration, predominantly low-skilled workers to Middle Eastern and Southeast Asian countries, was driven by limited domestic economic opportunities. Immigration was minimal, mainly some Rohingya refugees. Remittances from emigrants became crucial for the national economy.
Source: https://bangladesh.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1001/files/documents/Bangladesh-Migration-Report-2011.pdf (Covers historical trends)

Barbados
Emigration, including skilled professionals ("brain drain"), targeted opportunities in the US, UK, and Canada. Immigration included Caricom nationals for tourism and services, and some returning nationals. Economic conditions and government policies aimed to manage labor needs influenced flows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (See pages 81-84 for Barbados)

Belarus
Post-Soviet economic challenges and limited political freedoms spurred emigration, primarily to Russia for labor and Western countries. Immigration was low, mainly some ethnic Belarusians returning or transit migration. Economic ties with Russia strongly influenced labor movement.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/pdfid/4cb6cc122.pdf (IOM Migration Profile of Belarus, 2010)

Belgium
EU integration facilitated free movement, a key immigration driver from member states, alongside family reunification and asylum seekers (e.g., Congo, Balkans). Emigration of Belgians occurred for work/study within EU. Policy focused on integration and asylum management.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/belgium_migr_outlook-2011-11-en (Belgium chapter)

Belize
Emigration, often to the US, was driven by economic opportunities and family ties. Immigration from neighboring Central American countries (Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador) occurred due to their instability and Belize's agricultural labor needs. Regularization programs were a policy feature.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_in_belize_mp_2011_final_1.pdf (IOM Migration in Belize 2010 Profile)

Benin
Emigration, largely intra-regional within West Africa (e.g., Nigeria, Ivory Coast), was for economic and trade opportunities, with some skilled emigration to France. Immigration was primarily from neighboring Sahelian countries for agricultural labor and commerce. Economic factors were dominant.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/research/working/473447102/pbn-2007-134-migration-development-west-africa-case-study-benin-ghana.html (Comparative study including Benin)

Bhutan
Emigration was primarily the flight of Lhotshampa (ethnic Nepali) refugees in the early 1990s. Other emigration was minimal. Immigration was strictly controlled, mainly skilled labor from India for development projects. The refugee situation dominated emigration patterns.
Source: https://www.hrw.org/reports/2007/bhutan0507/ (HRW report on Bhutanese refugees, provides context on the exodus)

Bolivia
Economic hardship drove significant emigration, mainly to Argentina, Spain (especially 2000s), and the US. Immigration was low, primarily from neighboring countries or returning Bolivians. Spain's economic boom and subsequent crisis significantly impacted emigration flows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/bolivia_profile_2011_spa.pdf (IOM Migration Profile of Bolivia 2011, Spanish)

Bosnia and Herzegovina
The 1992-1995 war caused massive forced emigration (refugees). Post-war, emigration continued due to economic stagnation, while some refugee return occurred. Immigration was minimal, mainly international personnel. Shift from war-driven to economic emigration.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/publications/brochures/3ae6bcf50/state-worlds-refugees-2000-fifty-years-humanitarian-action-chapter-5.html (UNHCR SOWR 2000, Ch. 5 on Balkan crisis)

Botswana
Political stability and economic growth attracted skilled professionals and labor from neighboring African countries, particularly Zimbabwe during its crises. Emigration of Batswana was limited. Management of irregular migration from Zimbabwe became a key issue.
Source: https://www.gov.bw/sites/default/files/2020-02/Botswana%202011%20Census%20analytical%20Report%20Final.pdf (2011 Census, Ch. 5 on Migration)

Brazil
Economic volatility in the 1990s spurred emigration (Dekasseguis to Japan, others to US/Europe). Immigration included some skilled labor, investors, and South American migrants. Economic stabilization in the 2000s slowed emigration and slightly increased immigration.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/brazil-new-player-global-migration-game (MPI article with historical overview)

Brunei Darussalam
Oil wealth fueled demand for foreign labor in construction and services, attracting migrants mainly from Southeast Asia. Emigration by Bruneians was very low. Government policies tightly controlled labor immigration, maintaining reliance on foreign workers.
Source: https://www.oecd.org/migration/mig/34603973.pdf (OECD/ILO 2005 report on foreign workers in Brunei)

Bulgaria
Post-communist transition brought economic hardship, driving emigration to Western Europe and North America. EU accession (2007) significantly increased labor mobility to other EU states. Immigration was low, mainly some returning Bulgarians, students, and transit migrants.
Source: https://www.iom.bg/sites/default/files/publications/Extended_Migration_Profile_of_Bulgaria_EN_2015.pdf (Historical sections, e.g., Ch. 2)

Burkina Faso
High poverty drove substantial labor emigration, predominantly to neighboring Côte d'Ivoire for agriculture, and other West African countries. Immigration was minimal, mostly regional. Political instability in Côte d'Ivoire periodically caused return migration or redirection.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/burkina_faso_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Burundi
Decades of civil conflict, especially the 1993-2005 civil war, were primary drivers of mass refugee outflows to neighboring countries. Immigration was negligible except for some returning refugees during peace interludes. The Arusha Accords (2000) initiated slow returns.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe27920.html (UNHCR Burundi operations profile 2010, context on refugees)

Cabo Verde
A long tradition of emigration persisted due to limited domestic economic opportunities, with diasporas in Europe (Portugal, France) and the US. Immigration was minimal. Remittances were crucial. Efforts to engage the diaspora were noted.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mp_cape_verde_2009_en.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Cambodia
Post-conflict recovery and economic hardship drove labor emigration, primarily to Thailand and Malaysia. Internal displacement was also significant. Immigration was low, some returning refugees and limited foreign investment-linked personnel.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/cambodia_migration_profile_2011.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2011)

Cameroon
Economic crisis (early 1990s) and limited opportunities fueled emigration of skilled/unskilled workers, mainly to other African countries and Europe. Immigration included refugees from neighboring conflicts (e.g., Chad, CAR) and regional labor migrants.
Source: https://www.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl486/files/jahia/webdav/shared/shared/mainsite/activities/countries/profils_migr_cameroun_nov2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Canada
A points-based system drove skilled immigration from diverse global sources, alongside family reunification and refugee resettlement. Emigration of Canadians occurred, often to the US for economic reasons. Policy continually adapted to labor market needs and humanitarian commitments.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/canada_migr_outlook-2011-12-en (Canada chapter)

Central African Republic (CAR)
Recurrent political instability, coups, and armed conflicts drove significant refugee outflows and internal displacement, primarily to neighboring countries. Immigration was minimal, mainly regional traders or pastoralists, and humanitarian personnel. Conflict was the overwhelming determinant.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/protection/operations/49c798512/unhcr-country-operations-profile-2009-central-african-republic.html (UNHCR CAR Profile 2009)

Chad
Internal conflicts and refugee influxes from Sudan (Darfur), CAR, and Nigeria characterized Chad's migration. Emigration of Chadians was less significant, often regional. Hosting large refugee populations amidst its own instability was a major challenge.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/protection/operations/49c7984d2/unhcr-country-operations-profile-2009-chad.html (UNHCR Chad Profile 2009)

Chile
Post-dictatorship return migration occurred (early 1990s). Economic stability later attracted immigrants from Peru, Bolivia, Argentina. Emigration of Chileans, lower than previous decades, continued for professional opportunities. Chile transitioned towards net immigration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/chile_migr_outlook-2011-35-en (Chile chapter)

China
Economic reforms fueled massive internal rural-urban migration. International emigration increased (economic opportunity, family ties) to North America, Australia, Europe, Africa/Asia. Immigration was low but growing (foreign professionals, traders).
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/china-internal-migrant-magnet-and-growing-source-emigrants (MPI overview)

Colombia
Internal armed conflict and drug violence drove mass internal displacement and emigration (US, Spain, Venezuela, Ecuador) for asylum/economic reasons. Immigration was low (some Venezuelans, returning Colombians). Peak emigration: late 1990s/early 2000s.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/colombia_profile_2012_0.pdf (IOM Perfil Migratorio Colombia 2012, Spanish)

Comoros
Limited economic opportunities and political instability drove significant emigration, particularly to France (Mayotte, mainland). Immigration was minimal. Remittances from the diaspora were vital for the Comorian economy.
Source: https://www.un.org/esa/population/migration/publications/MigrationProfiles/Comoros.pdf (UN DESA Migration Profile 2009)

Congo, Democratic Republic of the (DRC)
Multiple, widespread, protracted armed conflicts (First/Second Congo Wars) caused immense internal displacement and massive refugee flows into neighboring countries. Emigration was overwhelmingly conflict-driven. Immigration negligible beyond some returning refugees during brief calms.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/publications/brochures/3ae6bcf50/state-worlds-refugees-2000-fifty-years-humanitarian-action-chapter-6.html (UNHCR SOWR 2000, Ch. 6 Great Lakes)

Congo, Republic of the
Civil wars (1993-94, 1997-99) drove significant emigration and internal displacement (to DRC, Gabon, France). Post-conflict, some return occurred, but economic emigration continued. Immigration included refugees from DRC, CAR, and some West African traders.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe1bb2.html (UNHCR RoC Profile 2010)

Costa Rica
Relative stability attracted immigrants, notably Nicaraguans (fleeing conflict/poverty) and North American/European retirees. Emigration of Costa Ricans was moderate, often for education/work in US. Management of Nicaraguan immigration was a key policy focus.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/costa_rica_profile_01_08_2010_web.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010)

Côte d'Ivoire
Formerly a major West African immigration hub, political instability (1999 coup, civil war 2002-2007) led to Ivoirian emigration and forced departure/return of immigrants. Immigration dramatically reduced; it became a refugee source.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/cote_divoire_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Croatia
The 1991-1995 war of independence caused significant refugee outflows and internal displacement. Post-war, economic emigration continued; refugee return was gradual. Immigration limited, mainly ethnic Croats from Bosnia and Herzegovina. EU accession prospects influenced policies.
Source: https://migracije.hr/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/Migration_Profile_Croatia_2010.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010)

Cuba
Strict emigration policies and economic hardship (post-Soviet "Special Period") led to emigration primarily to the US (often irregular) or family reunification. Immigration was negligible. The US "wet foot, dry foot" policy significantly shaped emigration.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/cuban-migration-policy-and-ideology (MPI article on policies/trends)

Cyprus
Island division shaped migration. Republic of Cyprus saw labor immigration for tourism/services (Asia, Eastern Europe), boosted by EU accession (2004). Emigration of Cypriots moderate. Northern part saw immigration from Turkey.
Source: https://www.prio.org/download/publicationfile/1651/Cyprus%20Conflict%20and%20Negotiation%20State%20of%20Play%20September%202010.pdf (PRIO Cyprus Report Sept 2010, gives political/demographic context)

Czech Republic (Czechia)
Post-Velvet Revolution, economic emigration to West. Immigration increased significantly (Slovakia, Ukraine, Vietnam for labor), then EU citizens post-2004 accession. Shifted from emigration to immigration country.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/czech-republic_migr_outlook-2011-13-en (Czech Republic chapter)

Denmark
EU membership facilitated labor mobility (immigration from EU states), alongside refugees and family reunification. Emigration of Danes (often skilled) occurred. Denmark implemented progressively stricter immigration/integration policies, influencing inflow compositions.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/denmark_migr_outlook-2011-14-en (Denmark chapter)

Djibouti
Strategic location and relative stability attracted refugees from Somalia, Ethiopia, Eritrea. Emigration from Djibouti limited. Ports and foreign military bases brought foreign workers. Significant transit migration towards Arabian Peninsula.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/djibouti_migration_profile_2011_en.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2011)

Dominica
Emigration of skilled individuals (nurses) driven by limited economic opportunities (to US, UK, Canada, Caribbean). Immigration low (returning nationals, lifestyle migrants). Economic citizenship programs began emerging.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 97-99 for Dominica)

Dominican Republic
Significant emigration (US, Spain) driven by economic hardship, family networks. Major destination for Haitian labor migrants (agriculture, construction), often precarious. Circular migration common.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/dominican_republic_mp_09.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

East Timor (Timor-Leste) (Independence 2002)
Indonesian occupation limited external migration pre-1999. Independence violence caused mass displacement to West Timor. Post-2002: refugee returns, nation-building, some skilled emigration (education/opportunities), limited aid worker immigration.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/admin/hcspeeches/4067f69f4/unhcr-action-east-timor-1999-2004-lessons-learned-future-emergencies.html (UNHCR on 1999-2004 operations)

Ecuador
Severe economic crisis (1999-2000) triggered mass emigration (Spain, US, Italy). Immigration included Colombian refugees and Peruvian migrants. Oil sector attracted some skilled foreign labor. Spain's labor demand was a major pull factor.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/ecuador_profile_2008_eng.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Egypt
Labor emigration to Gulf countries and Libya remained significant (economic needs), fluctuating with regional factors. Some skilled emigration to West. Immigration included Sudanese refugees, Palestinian and Iraqi communities.
Source: https://egypt.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1006/files/documents/IOM-Egypt-Migration-Profile-Report-2010-EN.pdf (IOM Egypt Migration Profile 2010)

El Salvador
Post-civil war (ended 1992), high emigration (mainly to US) continued, driven by economic hardship, emerging gang violence, and social networks. Remittances vital. Immigration minimal. US policies significantly impacted Salvadorans.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/el-salvador-despite-end-civil-war-emigration-continues (MPI article, 2006)

Equatorial Guinea
Oil discovery (mid-1990s) transformed it from emigration (repression/poverty) to labor immigration country (from neighboring Africa, some skilled expatriates). Emigration of nationals became less common.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/492ac9692.html (US State Dept HR Report 2008, gives context on foreign labor)

Eritrea
Post-independence (1993), mandatory indefinite national service, political repression, and lack of economic opportunity led to continuous large-scale emigration (asylum seekers) to Sudan, Ethiopia, and towards Europe/Israel. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://www.hrw.org/report/2009/04/16/service-life/state-repression-and-indefinite-conscription-eritrea (HRW "Service for Life" 2009)

Estonia
Post-Soviet independence saw initial emigration (ethnic Russians, Estonians to West). Economic development and EU accession (2004) later stabilized emigration, attracting some immigration (returning Estonians, EU citizens, CIS labor). Shift from net emigration towards balance.
Source: https://www.iom.ee/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/Estonian-Migration-Profile-2010.pdf (IOM Estonian Migration Profile 2010)

Eswatini (formerly Swaziland)
Persistent unemployment and poverty, compounded by HIV/AIDS, drove labor emigration, overwhelmingly to South Africa (mining, agriculture, domestic work). Immigration limited (earlier Mozambican refugees, skilled expatriates). Circular migration to South Africa key.
Source: https://www.gov.sz/images/Labour/Migration%20Policy%20Draft%20Final%20Report%20%20.pdf (Draft Migration Policy, background sections p.14+)

Ethiopia
Conflict (Eritrean-Ethiopian war), political instability, droughts, poverty drove emigration (refugees to neighbors; labor/students to Middle East, West). Hosted refugees (Somalia, Sudan).
Source: https://ethiopia.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1011/files/documents/IOM-Ethiopia-Migration-Profile-2011.pdf (IOM Ethiopia Migration Profile 2011)

Fiji
Political instability (coups in 1987, 2000, 2006) and limited economic opportunities drove significant emigration of skilled Indo-Fijians and others, primarily to Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and the US. Immigration remained low, mostly expatriates or returning residents. Brain drain was a persistent issue.
Source: https://www.mfat.gov.fj/images/documents/Fiji_National_Migration_Profile_2013.pdf (Fiji National Migration Profile 2013, see historical context section)

Finland
EU accession (1995) spurred some labor immigration from other EU states and continued humanitarian intake. Emigration of Finns was moderate, often for work/study. Post-Soviet collapse led to immigration of Ingrian Finns from Russia. Economic recession (early 1990s) initially increased emigration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/finland_migr_outlook-2011-15-en (Finland chapter)

France
Traditional immigration continued (family reunification, asylum from Africa/Asia, EU free movement). Emigration of French nationals (skilled workers, youth) increased towards other EU countries and North America. Policy focused on selective skilled immigration and integration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/france_migr_outlook-2011-16-en (France chapter)

Gabon
Oil wealth attracted labor immigration from West and Central African countries (e.g., Cameroon, Benin, Equatorial Guinea) and some skilled expatriates. Emigration of Gabonese nationals was relatively low, often for education or specialized work. Economic conditions heavily influenced inflows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/gabon_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010, French)

Gambia
The limited economic opportunities and political factors under Jammeh's rule drove emigration, particularly youth, towards Europe (often irregular routes via North Africa) and neighboring West African countries. Immigration was mainly regional, some returning Gambians.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/gambia_migration_profile_2012.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2012)

Georgia
Post-Soviet independence, conflicts in Abkhazia and South Ossetia, and economic collapse led to mass emigration, primarily to Russia and later Europe/US. Immigration was low, mainly returning Georgians and some transit. Conflict was the dominant early driver, then economic factors.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/georgia_migration_profile_2008_eng.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Germany
Reunification (1990) saw East-West internal migration and immigration of ethnic Germans ("Aussiedler") from Eastern Europe/FSU. Asylum seekers (Balkans) and EU labor migration (post-2004) were significant. Emigration of Germans also occurred. Policy shifted towards managing integration and skilled labor needs.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/germany_migr_outlook-2011-17-en (Germany chapter)

Ghana
Economic hardship in early period drove emigration (skilled/unskilled) to Europe, North America, and other African nations. Political stability later attracted some returning Ghanaians and regional immigrants (e.g., Liberian refugees). Shift from primarily economic emigration to more diverse flows.
Source: https://ghana.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1016/files/documents/Ghana-Migration-Profile-2009_0.pdf (IOM Ghana Migration Profile 2009)

Greece
Transitioned from an emigration to an immigration country. Mass immigration from Albania and other Balkan/Eastern European states post-Cold War (economic opportunities, proximity). EU accession facilitated some Greek emigration. Asylum seekers from Middle East/Asia increased.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/greece_migr_outlook-2011-18-en (Greece chapter)

Grenada
High emigration rates, particularly of skilled individuals ("brain drain") to the US, UK, Canada, and other Caribbean islands, driven by limited economic opportunities. Immigration was low, mainly returning nationals or some expatriates. Hurricane Ivan (2004) spurred temporary emigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 100-102 for Grenada)

Guatemala
Post-civil war (ended 1996), persistent poverty, violence, and lack of opportunities fueled large-scale emigration, primarily to the US (often irregular) and Mexico. Immigration was minimal, mostly regional. Natural disasters also spurred emigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/guatemala_country_profile_2011_eng.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2011)

Guinea
Political instability and economic hardship drove emigration to other West African countries and Europe. Guinea hosted significant refugee populations from neighboring Liberia and Sierra Leone due to their civil wars. Resource extraction attracted some foreign workers.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/guinea_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Guinea-Bissau
Chronic political instability, coups, and extreme poverty drove emigration, mainly to Portugal, Senegal, and other Lusophone/West African countries. Immigration was very limited, some regional traders. "Narco-state" issues exacerbated instability.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe2432.html (UNHCR Guinea-Bissau Factsheet 2010, provides operational context)

Guyana
Very high emigration rates ("brain drain" of skilled professionals and others) to US, Canada, UK, and Caribbean, driven by limited economic opportunities and social factors. Immigration was minimal, some returning Guyanese or Brazilian gold miners.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 103-106 for Guyana)

Haiti
Political instability, extreme poverty, and natural disasters (e.g., 2010 earthquake) drove continuous emigration, primarily to the Dominican Republic, US, Canada, and France. Immigration was negligible. The 2010 earthquake caused a massive new wave of displacement and emigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/haiti_migration_profile_2010_fr.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010, French – focuses on post-earthquake but gives context)

Honduras
Poverty, unemployment, natural disasters (Hurricane Mitch 1998), and rising gang violence fueled significant emigration, overwhelmingly to the US (often irregular). Immigration was minimal. Remittances became crucial for the economy.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/honduras-caught-perfect-storm (MPI 2006 article providing context)

Hungary
Post-communist transition saw some emigration. EU accession (2004) facilitated labor mobility (emigration to UK, Germany, Austria) and attracted immigration (ethnic Hungarians from neighbors, EU citizens, investors). Shift from minor emigration to more balanced flows.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/hungary_migr_outlook-2011-19-en (Hungary chapter)

Iceland
Economic boom attracted labor immigration (Poland, Baltics) until the 2008 financial crisis, which then spurred emigration of Icelanders and some immigrants. Pre-boom, migration flows were modest. Free movement in EEA was a factor.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/sites/b6d91203-en/index.html?itemId=/content/component/b6d91203-en (OECD Migration Outlook 2010, Iceland specific section, though the link is to 2010 general) (Better: https://www.statice.is/publications/publication/health-and-welfare/migration-2010/ - see tables/summaries for trends)

India
Significant labor emigration (skilled/unskilled) to Gulf, North America, Europe, Australasia, driven by economic opportunities. Internal migration also vast. Immigration included refugees (Tibetans, Sri Lankans) and some skilled foreign workers. IT boom fueled skilled emigration.
Source: https://www.oecd.org/india/48327972.pdf (OECD, "Indian migration to OECD countries", 2011, provides context)

Indonesia
Large-scale labor emigration (domestic workers, factory/plantation labor) to Malaysia, Middle East, East Asia, driven by economic need. Internal migration (transmigration program) significant. Immigration limited (expatriates, some refugees).
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/indonesia_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010)

Iran
Post-revolution brain drain and emigration due to political/social restrictions continued. Hosted large Afghan and Iraqi refugee populations. Economic sanctions impacted opportunities, influencing some emigration. Some labor immigration from Afghanistan.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe24f2.html (UNHCR Iran Operations Profile 2010, details refugee situation)

Iraq
Gulf War (1990-91), sanctions, internal repression, and the 2003 invasion/subsequent conflict caused massive waves of emigration (refugees, asylum seekers) to Syria, Jordan, Iran, West. Immigration negligible, except returning Iraqis and some foreign personnel post-2003.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/publications/brochures/3ae6bcf50/state-worlds-refugees-2000-fifty-years-humanitarian-action-chapter-7.html (UNHCR SOWR 2000, Ch.7 on Iraq; subsequent reports detail post-2003)

Ireland
Economic boom ("Celtic Tiger," mid-1990s-2008) transformed Ireland from net emigration to significant net immigration (EU labor, returning Irish). Emigration fell. Financial crisis (2008) reversed this, spurring renewed emigration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/ireland_migr_outlook-2011-20-en (Ireland chapter)

Israel
Continued immigration ("Aliyah") of Jews from FSU, Ethiopia, and other countries was central. Emigration of Israelis ("Yerida") for economic/personal reasons occurred. Palestinian movement severely restricted. Security situation influenced flows.
Source: https://www.cbs.gov.il/en/subjects/Pages/Immigration-and-Absorption.aspx (Israel CBS data portal; specific reports for years in range available, e.g. Statistical Abstract of Israel 2011, Ch.4 Migration)

Italy
Shifted from emigration to major immigration country. Immigrants from North Africa, Albania, Eastern Europe, Asia sought economic opportunities. EU membership facilitated free movement. Some Italian emigration continued (skilled to North Europe, US). Policy focused on managing inflows.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/italy_migr_outlook-2011-21-en (Italy chapter)

Jamaica
High rates of emigration, particularly skilled professionals ("brain drain") and family reunification, to US, UK, Canada, driven by economic opportunities and established diaspora networks. Immigration was low, mainly returning residents and some expatriates. Remittances were vital.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 107-110 for Jamaica)

Japan
Strict immigration policies meant low immigration, mainly some "Nikkeijin" (ethnic Japanese from Latin America), trainees, and skilled professionals. Emigration of Japanese was also low. Aging population began to create pressures for more foreign labor towards end of period.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/japan_migr_outlook-2011-22-en (Japan chapter)

Jordan
Hosted large Palestinian refugee population. Influxes of Iraqi refugees (1990s, 2000s) were major immigration drivers. Labor emigration of Jordanians to Gulf states. Inward labor migration from Egypt and South Asia for lower-skilled jobs. Regional instability key factor.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/jordan_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Jordan Migration Profile 2010)
Kazakhstan
Post-Soviet independence saw large emigration of ethnic Russians, Germans, Ukrainians. "Oralman" program encouraged ethnic Kazakh immigration. Oil boom attracted some skilled foreign labor. Shift from mass ethnic emigration to more managed labor/ethnic immigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/kazakhstan_profile_2010_eng.pdf (IOM Kazakhstan Migration Profile 2010)

Kenya
Emigration of skilled Kenyans (health, education) to West, Southern Africa. Hosted large refugee populations from Somalia, Sudan, Great Lakes. Economic opportunities limited, driving emigration. Some regional labor immigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/kenya_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Kenya Migration Profile 2010)

Kiribati
Limited emigration due to scarce opportunities abroad, mainly for seafaring, or educational programs (Australia/NZ). Climate change impacts began to be discussed as future driver. Immigration negligible. Extremely vulnerable to environmental change.
Source: https://www.mfed.gov.ki/sites/default/files/Kiribati%20Population%20Census%20Report%202010%20Volume%202.pdf (2010 Census, Vol 2, Ch. 4 Migration – very limited data but implies low international movement)

Korea, North (DPRK)
Extremely strict emigration/immigration controls. Emigration almost entirely clandestine defections (to South Korea via China). Immigration negligible, few exceptions (e.g., some ethnic Koreans from Japan in earlier periods, very limited official visitors).
Source: https://www.hrw.org/reports/2006/northkorea0306/ (HRW "A Life of No Choice", 2006, details escapees)

Korea, South (ROK)
Shift from net emigration to net immigration country. Industrialization attracted labor migrants (trainees, then Employment Permit System) from Asia. Emigration for education/work continued. Marriage migration (foreign brides) significant.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/korea_migr_outlook-2011-23-en (Korea chapter)

Kosovo (Declaration of independence 2008)
Conflict (1998-99) led to mass refugee outflows. Post-conflict, high unemployment and limited opportunities drove significant economic emigration, mainly to Western Europe (Germany, Switzerland). Immigration minimal (international personnel, some returning diaspora).
Source: https://ask.rks-gov.net/media/1593/migration-profile-of-kosovo-2008.pdf (IOM/UNDP Kosovo Migration Profile 2008)

Kuwait
Massive dependence on foreign labor (South/Southeast Asia, Arab countries) for all sectors, following expulsion of Palestinians/Jordanians post-1991 Gulf War. Emigration of Kuwaiti nationals very low. Kafala system governed labor.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, Kuwait country profile)

Kyrgyzstan
Post-Soviet economic decline and political instability (e.g., 2005, 2010 revolutions) drove large-scale labor emigration, primarily to Russia and Kazakhstan. Ethnic Russian emigration also occurred. Immigration minimal. Remittances became critical.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/kyrgyzstan_migration_profile_2010_eng.pdf (IOM Kyrgyzstan Migration Profile 2010)

Lao PDR (Laos)
Economic hardship and limited opportunities drove labor emigration, primarily to neighboring Thailand (often irregular, for agriculture/construction). Immigration was low, mainly some returning Laotians, Vietnamese business migrants, and development workers. Rural poverty was a key emigration driver.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/lao_pdr_migration_profile_2010_eng.pdf (IOM Laos Migration Profile 2010)

Latvia
Post-Soviet independence saw initial emigration (ethnic Russians, Latvians to West). Economic challenges and then EU accession (2004) significantly increased labor emigration to UK, Ireland, other EU states. Immigration low, some returning Latvians, EU citizens. Major demographic impact from emigration.
Source: https://www.iom.lv/images/files/Petijumi/Migration_Profile_of_Latvia_2009_en.pdf (IOM Migration Profile of Latvia 2009)

Lebanon
Post-civil war (ended 1990) reconstruction attracted Syrian labor. Lebanese emigration continued (skilled, family reunification to West, Gulf). Hosted Palestinian refugees. Israeli conflicts (e.g., 2006 war) caused displacement and temporary emigration. Volatile political/economic situation influenced flows.
Source: https://www.aub.edu.lb/ifi/Documents/publications/working_papers/2009-2010/20100301_wp1_international_migration_lebanon.pdf (AUB Working Paper, 2010, "International Migration and Migrant Workers in Lebanon")

Lesotho
Heavy reliance on labor emigration to South African mines, though this declined somewhat over the period due to changes in SA mining. High unemployment and poverty were primary drivers. Immigration was minimal. Circular migration was characteristic.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/lesotho_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Liberia
Brutal civil wars (1989-1997, 1999-2003) caused massive refugee outflows to neighboring countries and internal displacement. Emigration was overwhelmingly conflict-driven. Post-2003, gradual refugee return and some skilled diaspora return occurred. Immigration otherwise negligible.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/4d763c6a9.pdf (UNHCR Global Report 2010, Liberia section for return context)

Libya
Oil wealth attracted significant labor immigration from sub-Saharan Africa and Arab countries (Egypt, Sudan). Was a major transit country for migrants attempting to reach Europe. Emigration of Libyans was low. Gaddafi regime's policies heavily shaped migration.
Source: https://www.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl486/files/jahia/webdav/shared/shared/mainsite/media/docs/reports/Libya-Migration-Profile-Oct-2009.pdf (IOM Libya Migration Profile 2009)

Liechtenstein
High prosperity and demand for skilled labor drove immigration, mainly from Switzerland, Austria, Germany. Emigration of Liechtensteiners was very low. Strict immigration controls maintained a high foreign-born population percentage.
Source: https://rm.coe.int/fourth-report-on-liechtenstein/16808b55d6 (ECRI 2010 report, p.8-9 for population composition, implies drivers)

Lithuania
Post-Soviet independence saw initial emigration. EU accession (2004) led to large-scale labor emigration (UK, Ireland, Norway) due to economic disparities, causing significant demographic decline. Immigration remained low, mostly returning Lithuanians or some from CIS countries.
Source: https://www.iom.lt/data/Migration_profile_LT_2010_en.pdf (IOM Lithuania Migration Profile 2010)

Luxembourg
High economic prosperity and role as an EU capital drove significant immigration of skilled professionals, EU officials, and cross-border workers (France, Belgium, Germany). Emigration of Luxembourgers was low. Consistently high proportion of foreign residents.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/luxembourg_migr_outlook-2011-24-en (Luxembourg chapter)

Madagascar
Emigration was low, mainly skilled professionals/students to France. Immigration also low, some expatriates (French, Asian investors) and regional migrants. Political crises (e.g., 2002, 2009) periodically impacted stability and potentially some movement.
Source: https://www.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl486/files/jahia/webdav/shared/shared/mainsite/media/docs/reports/Madagascar-Migration-Profile-2010.pdf (IOM Madagascar Migration Profile 2010)

Malawi
Labor emigration, primarily to South Africa and other Southern African countries, driven by poverty and unemployment. Hosted refugees from Mozambique (early 1990s) and later from Great Lakes. Internal rural-urban migration significant.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/malawi_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Malaysia
Rapid economic growth fueled large-scale labor immigration from Indonesia, Bangladesh, Nepal, Philippines for manufacturing, construction, plantations, domestic work. Emigration of skilled Malaysians to Singapore, Australia, West occurred. Policies aimed to manage foreign labor.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/wcmsp5/groups/public/---asia/---ro-bangkok/documents/publication/wcms_160630.pdf (ILO "Labour migration from Indonesia to Malaysia", 2006, for context on key corridor)

Maldives
Tourism drove demand for foreign labor (Bangladesh, India, Sri Lanka) in hospitality and construction. Emigration of Maldivians was low. Concerns about climate change impacts on future migration began to surface.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/254/Report.pdf (GLMM South Asia, 2011, Maldives profile)

Mali
Long tradition of labor emigration, particularly to Côte d'Ivoire, France, and other West African countries, driven by poverty, environmental degradation, and limited opportunities. Hosted refugees from regional conflicts. Remittances important.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mali_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Malta
EU accession (2004) and location made it a frontline state for irregular maritime arrivals from Africa seeking asylum/transit to Europe. Emigration of Maltese low. Labor immigration for tourism/services increased.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/malta_migr_outlook-2011-36-en (Malta chapter)

Marshall Islands
Compact of Free Association with US allowed visa-free migration, driving significant emigration for economic opportunities, education, healthcare. Climate change concerns grew. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://www.doi.gov/sites/doi.gov/files/uploads/Marshall_Islands_Economic_Review_2009.pdf (US DOI Economic Review 2009, section on migration)

Mauritania
Emigration of Mauritanians for economic reasons to West Africa, Europe. Hosted refugees from Western Sahara and Mali. Expulsion of Black Mauritanians (late 1980s/early 1990s) led to refugee situations. Transit migration point for sub-Saharans towards Europe.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mauritania_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Mauritius
Some skilled emigration to Europe, Australia. Diversifying economy attracted skilled expatriates and some foreign labor. Government policies encouraged return of skilled diaspora and managed labor immigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mauritius_migration_profile_2010_en.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2010)

Mexico
Massive labor emigration to the US (documented and undocumented) was the dominant pattern, driven by economic disparities and established networks. NAFTA (1994) had complex impacts. Significant transit migration from Central America. Immigration into Mexico remained relatively low.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/mexico-era-declining-emigration-robust-transit-migration-and-immigration-policy-development (MPI "Mexico: An Era of Declining Emigration...")

Micronesia, Federated States of
Compact of Free Association with US allowed visa-free migration, driving significant emigration for economic opportunities, education, healthcare. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://www.doi.gov/sites/doi.gov/files/oga/compacts-of-free-association/upload/FSM-2010-Statistical-Yearbook.pdf (FSM 2010 Statistical Yearbook, p.31 on population/migration)

Moldova
Severe post-Soviet economic decline led to mass labor emigration, primarily to Russia and Western Europe (Italy, Portugal), often irregular. Significant demographic impact. Immigration very low. Remittances crucial.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/moldova_profile_2008.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Monaco
Extreme wealth and favorable tax laws drove immigration of affluent individuals and skilled labor (mainly from France, Italy). Emigration of Monegasques very low. A country with a majority foreign-born population.
Source: https://www.imsee.mc/Population-et-emploi (IMSEE, Monaco Statistics - census data shows composition. E.g., 2008 census)

Mongolia
Post-Soviet transition spurred emigration for education/work (South Korea, US, Europe, Japan). Some labor immigration (Chinese for construction). Rural-urban migration significant. Harsh winters (dzuds) sometimes exacerbated economic drivers for movement.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mongolia_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Mongolia Migration Profile 2010)

Montenegro (Independence 2006)
Balkan conflicts (1990s) caused displacement. Economic emigration to Western Europe continued. Post-independence, tourism development attracted some investment and seasonal labor. EU candidacy shaped policies.
Source: https://www.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl486/files/jahia/webdav/site/myjahiasite/shared/shared/mainsite/policy_and_research/migration_montenegro_country_profile_2008.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Morocco
Long-standing emigration to Europe (France, Spain, Italy, Netherlands, Belgium) for labor and family reunification continued. Became a major transit country for sub-Saharan Africans. Some immigration of sub-Saharans and European retirees.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/morocco-setting-sights-europe-and-beyond (MPI 2005 article with good historical context)

Mozambique
End of civil war (1992) led to mass refugee repatriation. Economic recovery attracted some skilled foreign workers and investment. Labor emigration to South African mines continued, though reduced. Transit migration through Mozambique.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/mozambique_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Myanmar (Burma)
Political repression, conflict, and lack of economic opportunity drove significant emigration (refugees, labor migrants) to Thailand, Malaysia, Bangladesh, India. Rohingya persecution led to major refugee flows. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://www.tni.org/files/publication-downloads/b42-myanmarmigration-briefing-eng.pdf (TNI "Myanmar's Migration Pains" 2008)

Namibia
Post-independence (1990), some return migration. Relatively stable, attracted some skilled Africans and expatriates. Emigration of Namibians (often skilled) to South Africa and elsewhere occurred. Management of Angolan refugee situation in early part of period.
Source: https://www.lac.org.na/projects/grap/Pdf/Displaced_Persons_and_Africa.pdf (LAC "Displaced Persons and Development in Southern Africa," 2003 - context on regional displacement, incl. Namibia)

Nauru
End of phosphate mining boom led to severe economic decline, prompting some emigration (to Australia for education/work). Previously hosted I-Kiribati workers. Asylum seeker processing for Australia (late 2000s) was a significant, though controversial, form of "immigration."
Source: https://www.aph.gov.au/About_Parliament/Parliamentary_Departments/Parliamentary_Library/pubs/rp/rp0102/02RP09 (Aus Parl Library "Offshore processing of asylum seekers..." 2001, provides early context on this policy)

Nepal
Poverty, limited domestic opportunities, and civil conflict (1996-2006) fueled massive labor emigration, primarily to India, Gulf countries, and Malaysia. Remittances became vital. Immigration minimal (some Tibetans, Bhutanese refugees earlier).
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/nepal_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Nepal Migration Profile 2010)

Netherlands
Continued to be an immigration country (family reunification, asylum, EU labor). Emigration of Dutch nationals also occurred (to neighboring EU, Australia, Canada). Policy emphasis shifted towards integration and stricter asylum/immigration controls.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/netherlands_migr_outlook-2011-25-en (Netherlands chapter)

New Zealand
Skilled migration programs attracted immigrants (UK, Asia, South Africa). Significant emigration of New Zealanders (especially to Australia for economic opportunity – "brain drain/gain" dynamic). Pacific Islander quota migration continued.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/new-zealand_migr_outlook-2011-26-en (New Zealand chapter)

Nicaragua
Post-Contra War (ended 1990), continued economic hardship and political instability drove emigration, primarily to Costa Rica and the US. Remittances important. Immigration low, some returning Nicaraguans.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/nicaragua_profile_2011_eng.pdf (IOM Nicaragua Migration Profile 2011)

Niger
Poverty, desertification, and food insecurity drove seasonal and long-term labor emigration to coastal West Africa (Nigeria, Côte d'Ivoire) and North Africa (Libya, Algeria). Major transit country for West Africans heading to Europe. Immigration minimal.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/niger_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Nigeria
Large-scale emigration of skilled professionals ("brain drain") and others to Europe, US, and other African countries, driven by economic hardship and political instability. Significant internal migration. Some immigration of West African laborers and refugees. Oil sector attracted expatriates.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/nigeria_migration_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Nigeria Migration Profile 2009)

North Macedonia (formerly Macedonia, FYR)
Post-Yugoslav breakup, economic challenges and inter-ethnic tensions (e.g., 2001 conflict) drove emigration (mainly Albanians and Macedonians to Western Europe). Immigration low. EU candidacy process influenced migration policies.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/macedonia_migration_profile_2010_eng.pdf (IOM North Macedonia Migration Profile 2010)

Norway
Oil wealth and high living standards attracted labor immigrants (EU/EEA, skilled from elsewhere) and asylum seekers. Emigration of Norwegians relatively low. Integration policies and management of oil revenues were key factors.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/norway_migr_outlook-2011-27-en (Norway chapter)

Oman
Heavy reliance on foreign labor (South Asia, other Arab countries) for construction and service sectors, driven by oil revenues and development plans. Emigration of Omanis very low. "Omanization" policies aimed to increase national workforce participation.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, Oman country profile)

Pakistan
Significant labor emigration (skilled/unskilled) to Gulf countries, Europe, North America, driven by economic needs. Hosted large Afghan refugee population for decades. Internal displacement due to conflict/disasters. Remittances crucial.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/pakistan_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Pakistan Migration Profile 2010)

Palau
Compact of Free Association with US allowed visa-free migration, driving significant emigration for economic opportunities, education, healthcare. Tourism attracted some foreign workers (Philippines).
Source: https://www.palaugov.pw/wp-content/uploads/2018/02/2005-Census-Monograph-Migration-Report.pdf (Palau 2005 Census Monograph on Migration, provides data and context)

Palestine (West Bank and Gaza Strip)
Ongoing conflict, occupation, and severe movement restrictions were primary drivers of forced displacement and emigration (to Jordan, Gulf, West). Immigration limited to some returnees or foreign spouses. Blockade of Gaza profoundly impacted mobility.
Source: https://www.un.org/unispal/document/auto-insert-184373/ (UNSCO Report on Palestinian Economy, 2009 – highlights movement/access restrictions impacting livelihoods and driving displacement)

Panama
Canal and services-based economy attracted some skilled labor and retirees (US, Europe, Latin America). Emigration of Panamanians moderate (US, Spain). Transit migration increased. Economic stability was a pull factor.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/panama_profile_2011_eng.pdf (IOM Panama Migration Profile 2011)

Papua New Guinea
Emigration low, mainly skilled professionals to Australia. Immigration also low, primarily expatriates for resource extraction (mining, logging) and development aid. Internal migration significant.
Source: https://pngnri.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/DP114_International-migration-and-the-labour-market-in-Papua-New-Guinea.pdf (NRI Discussion Paper, 2010, "International migration and the labour market in Papua New Guinea")

Paraguay
Economic hardship drove emigration, primarily to Argentina (labor) and Spain (boom years). Immigration low, some Brazilians (agriculture), Koreans, and Mennonites. Border regions with Brazil had dynamic flows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/paraguay_profile_2011_spa.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2011, Spanish)

Peru
Economic crisis (early 1990s) and internal conflict (Shining Path) drove mass emigration (US, Spain, Argentina, Chile, Japan - Nikkei). Immigration low, some border migration. Fujimori's policies also prompted some political exile.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/peru_profile_2012_0.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2012, Spanish)

Philippines
Systematic, large-scale labor emigration (seafarers, domestic workers, nurses, professionals) globally, driven by economic need and government policy. Remittances crucial. Immigration low, mainly expatriates, some retirees. Established as a major labor-sending state.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/philippines_profile_2008.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Poland
Post-communist transition saw some emigration. EU accession (2004) triggered massive labor emigration, especially to UK, Ireland, Germany, Netherlands, due to economic disparities. Immigration low, but growing from Ukraine, Belarus towards end of period.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/poland_migr_outlook-2011-28-en (Poland chapter)

Portugal
Transitioned from primarily emigration to also an immigration country. Immigration from Brazil, Lusophone Africa, Eastern Europe (Ukraine, Romania) for labor. Emigration of Portuguese continued, especially youth/skilled, increasing again with late 2000s crisis.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/portugal_migr_outlook-2011-29-en (Portugal chapter)

Qatar
Massive reliance on foreign labor (South Asia, Southeast Asia, Arab countries) for construction, services, driven by huge gas/oil wealth and rapid development (e.g. 2006 Asian Games). Emigration of Qataris extremely low. Kafala system dominant.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, Qatar country profile)

Romania
Post-communist economic hardship drove significant emigration (Italy, Spain). EU accession (2007) dramatically increased labor mobility to other EU states. Immigration low, mainly Moldovans, some students/business.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/romania_migr_outlook-2011-37-en (Romania chapter)

Russia (Russian Federation)
Post-Soviet collapse saw emigration of ethnic minorities (Jews, Germans) and brain drain. Became major destination for labor migrants from CIS countries (Central Asia, Caucasus, Ukraine, Moldova). Internal migration significant. Conflicts (Chechnya) caused displacement.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/russia_profile_2008.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Rwanda
Genocide (1994) caused massive refugee outflows and internal displacement. Post-genocide, large-scale refugee repatriation, some continuing Tutsi diaspora return. Emigration for economic/educational reasons. Immigration limited beyond returnees and regional traders.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/publications/brochures/3ae6bcf50/state-worlds-refugees-2000-fifty-years-humanitarian-action-chapter-6.html (UNHCR SOWR 2000, Ch. 6 Great Lakes, details initial crisis and returns)

Saint Kitts and Nevis
Emigration for economic opportunities (US, UK, Canada, other Caribbean islands), including skilled individuals. Immigration included returning nationals, expatriates, and increasingly, economic citizenship applicants. Tourism drove some labor demand.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 123-125 for St. Kitts and Nevis)

Saint Lucia
Emigration for economic/educational opportunities (US, UK, Canada, other Caribbean). Tourism development attracted some returning nationals and foreign workers. Brain drain a concern.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 126-128 for St. Lucia)

Saint Vincent and the Grenadines
High emigration rates (US, UK, Canada, Caribbean) due to limited economic prospects, particularly for skilled labor ("brain drain"). Immigration low, mainly returning nationals. Seasonal agricultural worker programs important.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 129-131 for St. Vincent and the Grenadines)

Samoa
Longstanding emigration to New Zealand (quota), Australia, US (American Samoa) for economic opportunities and family reunification. Remittances vital. Immigration minimal.
Source: https://www.spc.int/sites/default/files/2020-06/WP01_10_MigrationSamoa.pdf (SPC "Migration in Samoa: A country profile 2008")

San Marino
Low emigration, mostly to Italy for specialized work/study. Immigration also low, mainly Italians, attracted by economic stability and proximity. A small, stable population.
Source: https://unstats.un.org/unsd/demographic/products/dyb/dyb_2009-2010/Table04.pdf (UN Demographic Yearbook 2009-2010, Table 4 - shows population by country of birth, indicating small foreign-born pop mostly Italian)

Sao Tome and Principe
Limited economic opportunities drove emigration, primarily to Portugal, Angola, Gabon. Immigration minimal, some returning nationals. A small island state with few drivers for inward migration.
Source: https://www.un.org/esa/population/migration/publications/MigrationProfiles/SaoTomeAndPrincipe.pdf (UN DESA Migration Profile, 2009)

Saudi Arabia
Massive reliance on foreign labor (South/Southeast Asia, Arab countries, East Africa) for all sectors, driven by oil wealth. One of world's largest immigration destinations. Emigration of Saudis very low. Strict Kafala system. Hajj/Umrah pilgrimage a major temporary inflow.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, Saudi Arabia country profile)

Senegal
Long tradition of emigration (France, Italy, Spain, US, other African countries) for economic reasons and trade. Became a significant transit country for West Africans. Hosted refugees (Mauritania, Guinea-Bissau). Some immigration of traders.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/senegal_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009, French)

Serbia (Including period as part of FR Yugoslavia/Serbia and Montenegro)
Balkan wars (1990s) caused massive displacement (refugees/IDPs from Croatia, Bosnia, Kosovo) into Serbia, and emigration from Serbia (economic, draft evasion). Post-2000, continued economic emigration. EU prospects started shaping policy.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/serbia_profile_2008.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2008)

Seychelles
Some emigration of skilled Seychellois for opportunities abroad. Tourism-driven economy attracted foreign labor, particularly in hospitality and construction. Government policies managed labor immigration.
Source: https://www.nbs.gov.sc/downloads/category/41-population-and-housing-census (Seychelles National Bureau of Statistics, 2010 Census reports show foreign worker data)

Sierra Leone
Devastating civil war (1991-2002) caused massive refugee outflows to Guinea, Liberia and internal displacement. Emigration overwhelmingly conflict-driven. Post-war saw refugee repatriation and some skilled diaspora return. Otherwise, immigration minimal.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/news/latest/2004/7/40f2a5194/end-sight-sierra-leonean-refugee-chapter.html (UNHCR 2004 article on end of refugee chapter)

Singapore
High-growth economy drove massive demand for foreign labor: high-skilled professionals (global) and lower-skilled workers (Malaysia, South Asia, Philippines) in manufacturing, construction, domestic services. Significant emigration of Singaporeans too. Strict immigration policies.
Source: https://www.population.gov.sg/resource-files/publications/population-white-paper-2013.pdf (Singapore Population White Paper 2013, references historical trends)

Slovakia (Independent from 1993)
Post-independence and economic transition saw emigration. EU accession (2004) significantly increased labor emigration (Czech Rep, UK, Ireland, Austria). Immigration low, mainly Czechs, Ukrainians. Shift from emigration to more balanced flows with economic growth.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/slovak-republic_migr_outlook-2011-30-en (Slovak Republic chapter)

Slovenia (Independent from 1991)
Post-independence, relative stability. EU accession (2004) saw some labor emigration but also immigration from ex-Yugoslav republics (Bosnia, Serbia, N. Macedonia) for labor. Became a net immigration country.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/slovenia_migr_outlook-2011-31-en (Slovenia chapter)

Solomon Islands
Low international migration. Some emigration for education/seasonal work (Australia, NZ). Ethnic tensions (1998-2003) caused internal displacement but limited international emigration. Immigration mainly aid workers, expatriates.
Source: https://www.spc.int/sites/default/files/2020-06/WP05_10_MigrationSolomonIslands.pdf (SPC "Migration in Solomon Islands: A country profile 2008")

Somalia
State collapse and protracted civil war from 1991 onwards drove massive, continuous refugee outflows and internal displacement across the Horn of Africa and globally. Emigration was overwhelmingly conflict and famine-driven. Immigration non-existent beyond some returning refugees.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/publications/brochures/3ae6bcf50/state-worlds-refugees-2000-fifty-years-humanitarian-action-chapter-6.html (UNHCR SOWR 2000, Ch. 6 covers early Horn of Africa crisis; subsequent reports detail ongoing situation)

South Africa
Post-apartheid (1994), shift from net white emigration to significant immigration from across Africa (Zimbabwe, Mozambique, DRC, Nigeria) for economic opportunities and asylum. Skilled emigration of South Africans continued. Xenophobic violence a recurrent issue.
Source: https://www.statssa.gov.za/publications/Report-03-04-05/Report-03-04-052010.pdf (StatsSA "Documented immigrants in South Africa, 2010" - offers some data and context)

South Sudan (Independence 2011, part of Sudan in this period)
Decades of civil war with North Sudan (Sudan) drove massive refugee outflows to neighboring countries and vast internal displacement within Southern Sudan. Emigration was entirely conflict-driven. Some returns during 2005 CPA peace period.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/sd/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2018/02/Statistical-Yearbook-2009-Sudan.pdf (UNHCR Statistical Yearbook 2009, Sudan chapter, includes Southern Sudan data)

Spain
Transformed from a major emigration country to a major immigration destination. Economic boom (late 1990s-2007) attracted massive labor immigration (Morocco, Ecuador, Romania, Colombia, UK). Spanish emigration low until late 2000s crisis spurred youth emigration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/spain_migr_outlook-2011-32-en (Spain chapter)

Sri Lanka
Civil conflict (until 2009) drove Tamil emigration (asylum, family) to West, India. Labor emigration (domestic workers, low-skilled to Middle East, East Asia) continued due to economic needs. Post-2009, some IDP returns.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/sri_lanka_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Sri Lanka Migration Profile 2010)

Sudan (Including South Sudan during this period)
North-South civil war and Darfur conflict (from 2003) caused massive internal displacement and refugee flows to Chad, Egypt, Kenya, Uganda. Labor emigration (to Gulf). Hosted Eritrean, Ethiopian refugees. Conflict was the overwhelming driver of displacement.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/sd/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2018/02/Statistical-Yearbook-2009-Sudan.pdf (UNHCR Statistical Yearbook 2009, Sudan chapter)

Suriname
Significant emigration, primarily to the Netherlands, driven by economic opportunities and historical ties. Some immigration from Brazil (gold mining) and Guyana. Brain drain a concern.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 132-134 for Suriname)

Sweden
Continued humanitarian intake (Balkans, Iraq, Somalia) and EU labor migration. Emigration of Swedes moderate (work/study in EU, US). Policy focused on integration and managing asylum flows. Became more diverse.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/sweden_migr_outlook-2011-33-en (Sweden chapter)

Switzerland
High demand for skilled labor drove immigration from EU/EFTA countries (Germany, Italy, France, Portugal) and beyond. Bilateral agreements with EU facilitated free movement. Emigration of Swiss moderate. Consistently high foreign population.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/switzerland_migr_outlook-2011-34-en (Switzerland chapter)

Syria (Syrian Arab Republic)
Hosted large Iraqi and Palestinian refugee populations. Labor emigration to Gulf states and Lebanon. Some immigration of Arab nationals. Pre-2011, relatively stable but economic pressures existed. Seeds of later conflict were not yet primary migration drivers internationally.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe2ea2.html (UNHCR Syria Operations Profile 2010, details refugee hosting)

Taiwan (ROC)
Emigration for education/work (US, Canada, Australia). Labor immigration (factory, domestic, care work) from Southeast Asia (Philippines, Indonesia, Vietnam, Thailand) increased significantly. Marriage migration (foreign brides from China, SE Asia) also notable.
Source: https://www.immigration.gov.tw/media/30081/2010national-immigration-agency-statistical-yearbook.pdf (Taiwan NIA Statistical Yearbook 2010 - see relevant tables)

Tajikistan
Civil war (1992-1997) caused displacement and emigration. Post-war, extreme poverty drove massive labor emigration, overwhelmingly to Russia. Remittances became cornerstone of economy. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/tajikistan_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Tajikistan Migration Profile 2009)

Tanzania
Hosted large refugee populations from Burundi, DRC, Rwanda. Relatively low emigration of Tanzanians, some skilled to Southern Africa/West. Some regional labor immigration. Rural-urban migration significant.
Source: https://www.unhcr.org/tz/wp-content/uploads/sites/3 Tanzania Statistical Yearbook 2009.pdf (UNHCR Tanzania Statistical Yearbook 2009, for refugee data context)

Thailand
Major destination for labor migrants from Myanmar, Laos, Cambodia (construction, agriculture, fishing, services). Emigration of Thais (skilled/unskilled to East Asia, US, Middle East). Transit country. Policies attempted to regularize irregular migrants.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/thailand_migration_profile_2011_eng.pdf (IOM Thailand Migration Profile 2011)

Togo
Political instability and economic hardship drove emigration to other West African countries (Ghana, Nigeria, Côte d'Ivoire) and Europe (France, Germany). Hosted some refugees. Remittances important.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/togo_profile_2010_fr.pdf (IOM Togo Migration Profile 2010, French)

Tonga
High emigration rates to New Zealand, Australia, US, for economic opportunity and education, facilitated by family networks and some visa schemes. Remittances essential. Immigration minimal.
Source: https://www.spc.int/sites/default/files/2020-06/WP07_10_MigrationTonga.pdf (SPC "Migration in Tonga: A country profile 2008")

Trinidad and Tobago
Emigration of skilled professionals ("brain drain") to US, Canada, UK. Economic prosperity (oil/gas) attracted some immigration from Caribbean and Venezuela, and returning nationals. Management of irregular migration a concern.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/migration_caribbean_regional_overview_0.pdf (Pages 135-139 for Trinidad and Tobago)

Tunisia
Longstanding emigration to France, Italy, Germany for labor. Became a transit country for sub-Saharan Africans. Some European retirees immigrated. Economic conditions and youth unemployment were key emigration drivers.
Source: https://www.ins.tn/sites/default/files/publication/pdf/ANNUAIRE%20STATISTIQUE%20DE%20LA%20TUNISIE%202005-2009%20%28FRAN%C3%87AIS%29_0.pdf (Tunisian National Institute of Statistics, Statistical Yearbook 2005-2009, Migration section)

Turkey
Shift from primarily labor emigration (Germany, W. Europe) to also a major transit and immigration country (asylum seekers from Iraq, Iran, Afghanistan; CIS labor). EU accession talks influenced migration policy. Some Turkish emigration continued.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/turkey_migr_outlook-2011-38-en (Turkey chapter)

Turkmenistan
Highly restrictive regime limited both emigration and immigration. Some ethnic Russian emigration post-independence. Limited, state-controlled labor migration or student exchanges. Data scarce and unreliable.
Source: https://www.hrw.org/europe/central-asia/turkmenistan (HRW page on Turkmenistan, notes severe restrictions on freedom of movement as general context)

Tuvalu
Limited emigration for work (seafarers, NZ seasonal work scheme) or education. Climate change and sea-level rise became dominant concerns for future displacement/migration. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://www.spc.int/sites/default/files/2020-06/WP09_10_MigrationTuvalu.pdf (SPC "Migration in Tuvalu: A country profile 2008")

Uganda
Hosted large refugee populations (Sudan, DRC, Rwanda, Somalia). Emigration of Ugandans for work (Middle East, South Africa) and education. Post-conflict recovery in North saw IDP returns. Some regional labor immigration.
Source: https://uganda.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1056/files/documents/Migration-in-Uganda-A-Country-Profile-2010.pdf (IOM Uganda Migration Profile 2010)

Ukraine
Post-Soviet economic crisis drove massive labor emigration (Russia, Poland, Czech Rep., Italy, Portugal). Some ethnic Russian emigration. Immigration low, mainly returning Ukrainians or from other CIS countries. Transit migration increased.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/ukraine_migration_profile_2010_eng.pdf (IOM Ukraine Migration Profile 2010)

United Arab Emirates (UAE)
Extreme reliance on foreign labor (South Asia, SE Asia, other Arab, Africa) for all sectors, driven by oil wealth and rapid diversification/construction. One of world's highest foreign-born percentages. Emigration of Emiratis negligible. Kafala system.
Source: https://www.ilo.org/dyn/migpractice/docs/106/Report.pdf (GLMM - Gulf Labour Markets and Migration, 2009, UAE country profile)

United Kingdom (UK)
Continued as major destination for immigrants (EU free movement, Commonwealth ties, skilled workers, asylum seekers). Significant emigration of British citizens (Australia, Spain, US, EU). Policy focused on managed migration, points-based system, integration.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/united-kingdom_migr_outlook-2011-39-en (United Kingdom chapter)

United States of America (USA)
Remained world's top immigration destination. Economic opportunities, family reunification, diversity visa, and refugee resettlement drove immigration from Latin America, Asia, Europe, Africa. Significant irregular migration. Emigration of Americans moderate. Policy debates on border control/reform.
Source: https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/social-issues-migration-health/international-migration-outlook-2011/united-states_migr_outlook-2011-40-en (United States chapter)

Uruguay
History of emigration (Argentina, Spain, US) due to economic stagnation. Early 2000s crisis spurred new wave. Some immigration from neighboring countries and returning Uruguayans. Relatively low but persistent emigration.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/uruguay_profile_2011_spa.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2011, Spanish)

Uzbekistan
Post-Soviet economic hardship and authoritarian rule drove labor emigration, mainly to Russia and Kazakhstan. Some ethnic Russian/minority emigration. Strict government controls. Immigration negligible. Cotton harvest often involved forced internal labor mobilization.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/uzbekistan_migration_profile_2010.pdf (IOM Uzbekistan Migration Profile 2010)

Vanuatu
Limited emigration, mainly for seasonal work (NZ, Australia) and education. Immigration low, primarily expatriates for tourism and development. Vulnerability to natural disasters a factor.
Source: https://www.spc.int/sites/default/files/2020-06/WP10_10_MigrationVanuatu.pdf (SPC "Migration in Vanuatu: A country profile 2008")

Venezuela
Historically an immigration country (Europeans, Colombians). Early 1990s saw some economic emigration. Chávez era (from 1999) led to increasing emigration of professionals/middle class due to political polarization and economic decline (effects became more pronounced post-2010). Still received Colombian migrants.
Source: https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/venezuela-land-immigrants-becomes-land-emigrants (MPI 2006 article, highlights shift)

Vietnam
Continued post-war emigration (family reunification, asylum). Economic reforms (Đổi Mới) fueled labor emigration (Taiwan, South Korea, Malaysia, Middle East) and rural-urban migration. Marriage migration (to S. Korea, Taiwan) significant. Immigration low.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/viet_nam_migration_profile_2011.pdf (IOM Vietnam Migration Profile 2011)

Yemen
Hosted Somali refugees. Labor emigration to Gulf states (Saudi Arabia primarily), though affected by regional politics/conflicts. Internal displacement due to Sa'dah conflict. Economic hardship and instability were key drivers.
Source: https://www.refworld.org/docid/4b6fe2fa2.html (UNHCR Yemen Operations Profile 2010, details refugee hosting and IDP situation)

Zambia
Hosted refugees from Angola, DRC, Great Lakes. Some emigration of skilled Zambians (health, education to Southern Africa, UK). Economic challenges drove some labor migration. Copperbelt decline earlier impacted internal/regional flows.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/zambia_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Migration Profile 2009)

Zimbabwe
Severe political crisis and economic collapse (hyperinflation) from late 1990s/early 2000s caused mass emigration (professionals, general populace) primarily to South Africa, UK, Botswana, Australia. Shift from modest emigration to crisis-driven exodus. Immigration negligible.
Source: https://publications.iom.int/system/files/pdf/zimbabwe_migration_profile_2009.pdf (IOM Zimbabwe Migration Profile 2009)
